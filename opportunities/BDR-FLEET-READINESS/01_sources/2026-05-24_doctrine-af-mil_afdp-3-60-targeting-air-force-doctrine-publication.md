FOREWORD 
Doctrine embodies the fundamental principles by which military forces guide their actions 
in support of national objectives. It is a body of carefully developed authoritative ideas 
that establishes a common frame of reference for solving military problems. Airmen today 
are serving in a time of consequence marked by the rapid evolution of the character of 
war and increasingly formidable competitors challenging our freedom of maneuver and 
freedom of action. 
The United States Air Force (USAF) should advance solutions to deter, compete, and win 
by focusing on readiness and lethality . Air Force Doctrine Publication (AFDP)  3-60, 
Targeting, though firmly rooted in existing best practices, also looks to the future, adapting 
where needed to enable continued warfighting effectiveness. Targeting links ends, ways, 
means, and risk to ensure appropriate responses are aligned to operational objectives. 
The targeting process is inform ed by planning, intelligence,  and the air tasking cycle, 
ensuring alignment between operational objectives and the employment of airpower. 
This updated version of AFDP 3- 60, Targeting, provides Airmen with a shared 
understanding of targeting as a process and its importance in the modern battlespace. It 
outlines the fundamental principles and framework that guide the planning and execution 
of targeting. This publication also provides an overview of organizations with targeting 
responsibilities and air component-level processes for deconflicting and integrating target 
nominations and engagements across the joint force. 
AFDP 3-60 serves as a vital tool for Airmen at the operational level, equipping them with 
the knowledge to synthesize targeting processes across the spectrum of conflict -from 
deterrence and competition to large- scale combat operations. Throughout history, 
innovative Airmen have developed methods to meet operational requirements.  The 
airman’s perspective is fundamental to targeting due to airpower’s ability to create effects 
across both the theater and globe.  This doctrine reinforces the USAF’s ability to prevail 
amid complex challenges.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION TO TARGETING 
Targeting is the process of selecting and prioritizing targets, and matching the appropriate 
response to them, considering operational requirements and capabilities.1 While targeting 
integrates all joint military capabilities  to achieve the objectives of the joint force 
commander (JFC), the process is heavily shaped by the air component. The unique 
speed, range, flexibility, and precision of airpower make it a primary tool for creating 
desired effects against a wide array of t argets, thus influencing how targets are 
developed, prioritized, and engaged. This process is n ot the exclusive province of one 
specialty or division, such as intelligence or operations, but blends the expertise of many 
disciplines to leverage the decisive capabilities the air component brings to the joint force. 
The targeting process unfolds as a continuous, analytic, and integrative cycle that occurs 
at the intersection of intelligence, planning, and operations. The targeting process is not 
a standalone activity but is deeply integrated with and dependent upon these other 
processes. Targeting relies on intelligence for the detailed information necessary for 
target development, vetting, and assessment.  In turn, t argeting receives direction from 
the planning proces s, which provides commander’s objectives, guidance, and desired 
effects. Outputs of the targeting process  feed operations by providing the necessary 
inputs for mission planning and execution of the air tasking cycle. Subsequently, the 
execution of air operations provides feedback into the intelligence and targeting 
processes through assessment, allowing for continuous refinement of the overall effort.  
This complex and continuous interplay of intelligence, planning, operations, and targeting 
contributes to the success of the air component and the joint force. 
FUNDAMENTALS OF TARGETING 
The purpose of the targeting process is to create desired effects in the operational 
environment (OE) to support the achievement of commanders’ objectives by prioritizing, 
integrating, synchronizing, and applying fires and other capabilities.
2 To accomplish this 
purpose, the air component relies on several fundamentals. It adheres to the established 
principles of targeting, applies them  across the entire competition continuum, integrates 
capabilities and effects in all domains, operates with global  integration, and conducts all 
actions in accordance with the rule of law. 
PRINCIPLES OF TARGETING 
Five fundamental principles guide the air component’s targeting process. The first 
principle, objectives-based targeting, dictates that the entire process focus es on 
achieving the JFC’s objectives and that all targets nominated for engagement should 
contribute in a discernible way to attaining the JFC’s desired end state. Effects -based 
targeting emphasizes that effects are created or generated to achieve objectives. Effects 
are primarily labeled as lethal or nonlethal , but can also be classified as desired or 
undesired, intended or unintended,  direct or indirect , cumulative, and cascading.3 This

TARGETING WITHIN THE COMPETITION CONTINUUM 
The air component contributes to the joint targeting process throughout the competition 
continuum. The competition continuum describes three broad categories of strategic 
relationships among actors in the OE: cooperation, competition below armed conflict, and 
armed conflict/war.6 Much of the targeting process can occur well prior to armed conflict, 
especially target development conducted in support of planning. During cooperation, air 
component targeting efforts can include building joint capability and capacity through 
supporting joint exercises  or humanitarian assistance and disaster relief operations. 
Within competition below armed conflict, the focus shifts to deterring adversaries and 
understanding their networks and decision- making processes with an emphasis on 
matching nonlethal effects to cyberspace or information capabilities. The transition to 
armed conflict requires a robust process to analyze , integrate, and prioritize targets for  
lethal and nonlethal effects. The foundational work done during the cooperation and 
competition phases, such as target development and target system analysis, can enable 
rapid, effective application of airpower when required, ensuring the joint for ce achieves 
decisive results. 
GLOBAL INTEGRATION 
Global integration enables the joint force to act in a coordinated manner against 
transregional adversaries and threats. Since airpower can deliver effects across vast 
distances, a target’s geographic location is often less important than the adversary 
system to which it belongs. Th is global integration  is managed through 
supported/supporting command relationships, guidance from the Secretary of War  
(SecWar), and the Global Integrated Target List (GITL). 
The GITL is an adversary -focused, rather than a geographically oriented, list of targets .

domains, including space, cyberspace, and the electromagnetic spectrum.  The air

ENTITY TYPES 
An entity performs a function for the adversary or enemy. Its location, purpose, or use 
can contribute to an adversary’s overall warfighting or war -sustaining capability. During 
the targeting process, entities are analyzed by intelligence and considered for 
development into targets. Entities are typically grouped into one of five types (FIVE-O).
10 
Facilities. A physical structure, group of structures , or area that performs one or more  
functions contributing to an adversary’s capability . These measurable geophysical 
parameters define the real property boundary and ensure no target duplication or 
boundary conflicts break unique identification. 
Individuals. A person (or p ersons) who provides one or more critical functions that 
contribute to an overall adversary capability. This type of entity  should be reserved for 
personnel who perform unique functions rather than serve as an individual part of a larger 
group. 
Virtual. Typically, a nontangible entity within cyberspace that enables equipment to 
function is a virtual element. This could include a collection of programs, files, websites , 
or personas that represent a physical unit, person, location, or event. 
Equipment. An object is made up of any number of devices and components, integrated 
and codependent, upon which engagement of one component results in an effect upon 
the whole. For instance, mass -produced equipment (tanks, aircraft, trucks) are entities 
associated with a larger system to which they contribute. 
Organizations. A group or unit that provides a function contributing to an adversary’s 
capability. Elements could include facilities, individuals, equipment, and virtual entities;  
however, this target type is delineated by the overall function the organization provides. 
TARGET CHARACTERISTICS 
A characteristic is any distinctive, intrinsic, or acquired feature that forms the basis of 
target development. Characteristics are used to describe an object, to provide context, 
and to support analysis by identifying similar or related data. In general, targets are 
defined by five characteristics: physical , functional, cognitive control and information , 
environmental, and temporal.
11 
Physical. These are features discernible to one or more of the five senses or through 
sensor-derived signature, which describe a target ’s physical nature. They may 
significantly affect the types and numbers of weapons and the methods employed against 
the target. 
Functional. These are features that describe what the target does  and how it does it. 
They describe the target’s function within the system, how the target or system operates, 
the target’s level of activity, the status of its functionality, and its importance to the enemy.

factors can be critical to creating desired effects, especially when targeting a system’s 
central controlling function. As with functional characteristics, these are often difficult to

inputs, information processing capacity, control functions , patterns and signatures , and

Environmental. These describe the effect of the environment on the target and its 
surroundings. They include factors such as weather, terrain, camouflage, and the civilian 
environment, all of which can influence engagement tactics and weapon selection. 
Temporal. These describe the target’s vulnerability to detection, engagement, or attack 
in relation to a time horizon. Because target priority and accessibility can change rapidly, 
this time factor can help determine when and how to find or engage a target. By 
comparing this factor to information latency and knowledge of friendly capabilities, the 
staff can make better recommendations regarding possible actions. 
PROCESSES, CATEGORIES, TYPES, SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS 
Airmen involved in the targeting process should understand how targets are processed, 
categorized, and prioritized. The standard targeting framework revolves around the two 
core processes (deliberate and dynamic), the categories and types of targets managed 
within them, and the special considerations applied to targets of unique importance. 
DELIBERATE TARGETING (PLANNED) 
Deliberate targeting is the systematic process for planned targets, the category of targets 
detected, identified, and developed in sufficient time to schedule actions against them in 
a specific tasking cycle. Planned targets are split into two types: scheduled and on call. 
Scheduled Targets. These are planned  targets upon which fires or other actions are 
scheduled for prosecution at a specified time.  These targets appear on an air tasking 
order (ATO) to synchronize actions with a specific delivery time or time on target. Diverting 
capabilities from scheduled targets could negatively impact desired effects during the 
execution period and should be weighed against the commander’s targeting guidance.

On-call Targets. These are planned target s upon which fires are determined using the 
deliberate targeting process and executed using the dynamic targeting process. This 
means these targets have dedicated capabilities listed against them on the ATO ; 
however, no specific delivery time or time on target is identified. The commander expects 
to find, fix, and track targets during the execution period to prosecute them. 
DYNAMIC TARGETING (TARGETS OF OPPORTUNITY) 
The dynamic targeting process prosecutes targets identified too late or not selected for 
action in time to be included in deliberate targeting. The category of targets for this 
process is called “targets of opportunity.” The term “dynamic target” is improper and not 
a type of target . Targets of opportunity are split into two types : unscheduled and 
unanticipated. 
Unscheduled Target. These are t argets of opportunity known to exist in the operating 
environment. Unscheduled targets may have had the same amount of target development 
as planned targets and reside on the joint target list (JTL) or the restricted target list (RTL); 
however, they were neither nominated nor approved for inclusion on the joint integrated 
prioritized target list (JIPTL) or  were not available for engagement within the execution 
period. Changes to the target status based on priority, access, or permissions could result 
in the need or opportunity to engage the target during the current operations cycle.
 
Unanticipated Target. These are t argets of opportunity that were  unknown or 
unexpected in the operating environment. These targets may not have been developed 
in the deliberate targeting process nor included on a JTL or RTL. Nevertheless, an 
evaluation of the candidate target is required before it can be engaged.

High-Value Target (HVT). A target the enemy requires for the successful completion of

during execution of the air tasking cycle, is often used to specifically engage TSTs. 
Component-Critical Target (C CT). These targets are HPTs that pose a threat to their 
mission or force for cross-component execution. They are considered a type of HPT and 
are nominated by Component commanders as TSTs.

LEGAL CONSIDERATIONS 
NOTE: This section is not all-encompassing and is no substitute for legal advice from the 
appropriate staff judge advocate (SJA)  office or designated judge advocate. Constant 
coordination between planners, operators, and judge advocates is essential. 
Commanders, planners, operators, and targeteers must understand the pertinent legal 
framework to comply with the laws and policies, t he application of which may be 
challenging given the nature of non- kinetic operations (e.g., ubiquity of cyberspace 
operations, authorities across multiple areas of responsibility (AOR)  and domains, or 
regional effect of information activities), irregular and modern warfare, and the often 
geographic orientation of domestic and international law.
14 
Legal authorities, principles, and international legal obligations directly affect all phases 
of targeting. Those involved in targeting should include their legal advisors from the 
beginning of the targeting process and seek counsel to obtain a thorough understanding 
of their obligations as they apply to targeting analysis. The legal and policy considerations 
impacting targeting include: 
 Basic principles of the LoW. 
 Specific LoW concepts concerning personnel, objects, places, and collateral damage 
minimization. 
 ROE considerations. 
 Civilian harm mitigation and response. 
 The early and continuous involvement of judge advocates in targeting. 
Targeting must adhere to the LoW and all applicable rules of engagement (ROE). It is the 
policy of the Department of War (DoW) to comply with the LoW “during all armed conflicts, 
however characterized.” Every Airman must comply with Lo W, particularly its basic 
principles explained below, and its requirements for the humane treatment of detainees. 
LoW encompasses all international law for regulating the conduct of hostilities binding on 
the US, including treaties and other international agreements to which t he US is a party, 
as well as applicable customary international law.15 Military necessity does not authorize 
or justify acts that are otherwise prohibited by Lo W. Instead, military necessity must  be 
applied in conjunction with other LoW principles. 
BASIC PRINCIPLES OF THE LAW OF WAR 
LoW rests on five fundamental principles that are inherent to all targeting decisions: 
military necessity, distinction (discrimination), proportionality, unnecessary suffering 
(humanity), and honor (chivalry).

SPECIFIC TARGETING RESTRICTIONS 
Targeting restrictions under LoW prohibit intentional attacks on civilians, civilian 
populations, and protected entities such as the wounded, sick, prisoners of war, medical 
units, religious and cultural sites, and civilian objects unless they are recharac terized as 
military objectives due to their use or function.  The Geneva Conventions mandate clear 
markings for protected sites, and commanders must balance military advantage with 
minimizing collateral damage, including environmental harm, by implementing strategies 
to mitigate adverse effects during and after conflict.
16 17 
Regarding the protection of civilian objects specifically, c ivilian objects may not be 
intentionally targeted for attack. However, civilian objects can be recharacterized as  
military objectives if, by their nature, location , purpose, or use, they make an effective 
contribution to military action and their total or partial destruction, capture, or 
neutralization offer a concrete and definite military advantage.  Use also refers to the 
object’s current function, which is one of the primary target characteristics within both joint 
and USAF targeting doctrine.  “Dual-use” facilities and objects serve both a military and 
civilian function. Though from a legal perspective,  such objects cannot be both civilian 
objects and military objectives. That is, if an object is considered a military objective per 
the LoW two- part test, it is not a civilian object.
18 Dual-use targets may  also require a 
higher level of approval authority due to their potential impact on the civilian population. 
RULES OF ENGAGEMENT 
Rules of Engagement (ROE). ROE are a commander’s tool to regulate the use of armed 
force in military operations. ROE provide a framework that encompasses national policy 
goals, mission requirements, and the law. ROE are directive in nature and must be issued 
by competent military authorities. Essentially, ROE are rules for a particular operation that 
govern the use of force to reflect the will of the civilian and military leadership.  ROE 
constrain the actions of US military forces to ensure their actions ar e consistent with 
domestic and international law, national policy, and objectives. Although ROE are not law, 
they are authoritative restrictions issued at the appropriate level of command to control 
the use of force. ROE may be more restrictive than LoW for a given situation, but they 
cannot be more permissive than allowed under Lo W; therefore, compliance with ROE 
should guarantee compliance with LoW. 
Standing ROE (SROE). ROE may be found in the S ROE, a CCDR’s theater-specific 
ROE, and ROE issued specifically for an operation. SROE  are contained in a classified 
Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Instruction (CJCSI) . The SROE provide 
implementation guidance on the inherent right of self-defense and the application of force 
for mission accomplishment. The SROE also provides a framework for the development 
and implementation of ROE across the competition. The important  point to remember is 
that the SROE are not tailored to s pecific military operations. They provide guidance in 
the absence of operation- specific ROE and do not include  targeting restrictions or

ROLE OF THE JUDGE ADVOCATE 
Judge advocates assist the commander, planners, and operators with reviewing targets 
for compliance with applicable LoW  and ROE restrictions (including collateral damage 
and other CCDR restrictions) before mission execution. Judge advocates also provide 
legal advice to commanders and their staffs on the conduct of military operations ensuring 
consistency with international and domestic legal obligations and the governing ROE. 
Legal advice and counsel are  necessary to develop, interpret, modify, and properly  
implement the ROE. Judge advocates and their support staff are trained, operationally 
oriented, and readily accessible to assist commanders, planners, and operators with legal 
considerations and ROE-related issues. Judge advocates should be engaged early and 
often in the targeting process.

CHAPTER 2: ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES 
The targeting process requires robust direction and organization to enable the conduct of 
campaigns and operations across the competition continuum. The targeting process is a 
command responsibility with support from many organizations. Targeting occurs from the 
combatant command (CCMD) level to the tactical unit level. Across this organizational

STAGE 1: OBJECTIVES, EFFECTS, AND GUIDANCE 
Prior to this stage occurring, the continuous joint targeting cycle  integrates the other 
processes of planning, intelligence, and operations to generate joint force guidance and  
develop targets. During this time, the air component develops a JAOP that supports the 
overall JFC objectives. These objectives provide focus for staff at all stages of the air 
tasking cycle and give targeting personnel the overarching purpose for their efforts. 
To implement guidance, the JFC establishe s a formal operational battle rhythm. This 
series of recurring meetings and working groups provides key touchpoints to integrate the 
targeting process across the joint force. Two such forums are the JTCB and JCMB. 
Joint Targeting Coordination Board (JTCB). The JTCB is a group formed by the JFC 
to perform broad targeting oversight functions , typically chaired by the deputy JFC, the 
J-3, or a component commander. The JTCB provides a forum where  components 
synchronize their targeting priorities and activities with their respective schemes of 
maneuver, ensuring all efforts are integrated to meet the JFC’s objectives. 
During this stage, representatives from the AOC Strategy Division (SRD) attend the JTCB 
to provide input on air component operations and, in turn, receive JFC guidance, intent, 
and apportionment decisions. Output from the JTCB will start development of the AOD.

expressed as a percentage or priority, to be devoted to the various air operations for a

STAGE 2: TARGET DEVELOPMENT 
The bulk of target development is completed by phase 2 of the continuous joint targeting 
cycle. In the time-bound air tasking cycle, the air component receives targeting guidance 
along with the joint target list (JTL) , the restricted target list (RTL) , and the no- strike list 
(NSL) from the JTCB. A JTL is a list of validated targets with  no target engagement 
restrictions, whereas the RTL contains validated targets with engagement restrictions. 
The NSL is not a target list, but contains a list of entities that are protected from the effects 
of military operations under international law or ROE. 
Component target nomination list  (TNL). Guided by the desired effects listed in the 
AOD, the targeting effects team (TET) within the combat plans division (CPD) analyzes 
the menu provided by the JTL and RTL and avoids entities listed on the NSL. The TET 
coordinates with other AOC teams within the information warfare team  (IWT) and the 
ISRD to select targets that best achieve the guidance listed in the AOD. This collaborative 
effort results in the creation of the air component target nomination list (TNL) . Once 
approved by the air component commander, the TNL is sent to the joint fires element 
(JFE) for inclusion in the joint integrated prioritized target list (JIPTL). The JIPTL is a 
prioritized list of targets approved by the JFC that feeds the ATO process. After the JTCB 
review, the JFC approves the JIPTL.

STAGE 3: WEAPONEERING AND ALLOCATION 
Weaponeering and allocation are two aspects of the air tasking cycle that function 
together to produce the MAAP  and are integral to targeting. During this stage, the 
approved JIPTL is pushed to the air component by the JFE. However, coordination for 
both weaponeering and allocation commences before th is final J IPTL approval and 
continues into execution planning (stage 5). 
Weaponeering. This is the process of determining the specific means required to create 
a desired effect on a given target.  Weaponeering identifies the whole range of 
engagement options that may affect the target and highlights or selects the most 
appropriate engagement capabilities commensurate with desired effects for each phase 
of the campaign. Targeteers quantify the expected results of kinetic and non- kinetic 
capabilities employed against prioritized targets to create desired effects. This results in 
probable outcomes over many replications of an event. It does not predict the outcome 
of every munition  delivery but represents statistical averages based on modeling, 
weapons tests, and real-world experience. Weaponeering is normally done by the Targets 
and Tactical Assessment (T TA) team from the ISRD  using validated data and 
methodologies (see Appendix A).

DELIBERATE VS DYNAMIC TARGET PROCESSES IN THE AIR TASKING CYCLE 
Although the dynamic targeting process occurs during the execution stage of the air 
tasking cycle, targets prosecuted by dynamic targeting are still processed through all six 
stages. The Deliberate vs Dynamic Targeting in Air Tasking Cycle diagram highlights the 
similarities and differences between the deliberate and dynamic targeting processes 
across target types and the air tasking cycle stages. The target type most associated with 
dynamic targeting is the unanticipated target. However, most targets prosecuted via 
dynamic targeting fall into the on- call or unscheduled target categories. On- call targets 
are included in the ATO along with the planned targets, but since the coordinates are 
unknown, no precise engagement time is listed. Unscheduled targets are  developed 
similarly to on-call targets but do not meet the priority to make the ATO and are accounted 
for in planning in case they are detected (thus the change in doctrine from “unplanned” to 
“unscheduled” targets). Overall, both deliberate and dynamic targeting adhere to target 
development standards throughout the six stages of the air tasking cycle.  However, 
during the dynamic targeting process, target development is accomplished with 
compressed timelines.  Targets engaged through dynamic targeting may not be 
characterized to the same level of detail before prosecution as occurs with deliberate 
targeting. Thus, a target should be considered fully developed when sufficient target 
intelligence and legal reviews support engagement.

APPENDIX A: TARGETING AUTOMATION 
Inputting, updating, and verifying the vast amount of data needed to execute every stage 
of the targeting cycle requires automation to function successfully. Targeting automation 
leverages technological advances in software coding, computing, and network bandwidth 
to support the joint targeting process more efficiently and effectively . Targeting 
automation uses artificial intelligence and machine learning systems, applications, and 
database technologies to accelerate intelligence-driven development, data management, 
and information accessibility, systematically linking objectives and guidance to the target 
information and assessments required. 
Two foundational elements of targeting automation are common target data standards 
and data interoperability. These elements enhance the ease of information sharing 
amongst components, joint force elements, federated and reachback organizations, and 
multinational partners. This emphasis on sharing allows targeting automation to facilitate 
real-time, virtual, collaborative, and multilayered security analysis and planning. 
Finally, target automation is necessary to achieve the mass and speed required for 
targeting in a peer conflict. Targeting automation architecture should accommodate data 
transfer in contested, degraded, or operationally limited environments. It should also 
evolve to include both kinetic and non-kinetic capabilities to more fully exploit an effects-
based approach to operations.
