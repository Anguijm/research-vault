## Summary

The Department of the Navy's annual long-range shipbuilding plan, submitted to Congress pursuant to Section 231 of Title 10, United States Code. Released May 2026 and signed by Acting Secretary of the Navy Hung Cao, Chief of Naval Operations Admiral Daryl Caudle (the 34th CNO), and Commandant of the Marine Corps General Eric M. Smith (the 39th CMC). Frames the plan as the implementation of President Trump's Executive Order 14269 ("Restoring America's Maritime Dominance") and the subsequent Maritime Action Plan, under the banner of the "Golden Fleet Initiative."

Total new-construction funding programmed in the FY27-FY31 Future Years Defense Program is $268.1 billion across 75 ships. FY27 alone is $54.6 billion across 18 ships. The plan projects a battle force of 288 ships in FY27 growing to 398 by FY56, with the statutorily required 355-ship Navy reached at FY40.

Two sections of this document are load-bearing for the BDR-FLEET-READINESS research track. Page 34 ("Ship Repair and Maintenance") describes the Navy's planned shift from one-shot maintenance availabilities to a maintenance continuum, the application of AI / machine learning to predictive maintenance via ship digital twins, the consolidation of NAVSEA Industrial Operations Directorate plus the four public Navy shipyards plus all Navy Regional Maintenance Centers under what the document calls "PAE Industrial Operations" (terminology that may overlap with but is not obviously the same as the Amentum-owned PAE Industrial Operations contractor — see verification note below), and an explicit Navy statement that "the Navy and Marine Corps must have the right to repair their own equipment." Appendix 4 ("Planned Ship Decommissioning, Dismantling and Disposals") includes USS FITZGERALD (DDG 62) — the platform used in the first Japan SWARMEX — scheduled for inactivation in FY2030 with a Logistics Support Asset disposition.

## Extracted content — load-bearing sections

### Foreword from the Chief of Naval Operations (Admiral Daryl Caudle, 34th CNO, page 3)

> "The United States Navy exists to fight and win our Nation's wars at sea. The Fleet is our most decisive instrument of national power, and its strength begins in the Foundry, is delivered through the Fleet, and is proven in the Fight. This 30-year shipbuilding plan reflects the Navy's warfighting framework as articulated in the US Navy Fighting Instructions in order to execute the Navy Warfighting Concept, and it aligns our investments to ensure the Fleet we build is ready for the conflicts we may face, not just the ones we expect."

> "We are operating in an era defined by persistent competition, rapid technological change, and adversaries who are expanding their naval forces at scale. Because the future fight cannot be predicted with certainty, our approach is guided by the Hedge Strategy that builds a balanced, adaptable force designed to maintain decision advantage, preserve escalation dominance, and prevail across the full spectrum of operations, from daily campaigning to high-end combat."

### State of U.S. Navy Shipbuilding (page 6)

> "The Department of the Navy currently operates 291 battle force ships, while the Navy requirement by law is 355. Over the past two decades, the shipbuilding budget has doubled, yet we have no more ships now than in 2003. This is a persistent problem and one that is not just industrial. It is structural and the result of how we buy, how we plan, and how we manage risk in Navy acquisition."

> "The Navy's acquisition system grew administratively, but eroded operationally. Requirements expanded during execution, mature designs were modified and evolved by the Navy, and cost estimates and construction schedules were overly optimistic. As a result, schedules slipped, costs compounded, and the American taxpayer absorbed the bill while Sailors, Marines, and the Department of the Navy's readiness are left materially impacted."

### Distributed shipbuilding goal (page 8)

> "Today, roughly 10% of shipbuilding work is performed at distributed sites. Our goal is 50%."

### SHIP REPAIR AND MAINTENANCE — full section (page 34, load-bearing for this research track)

> "Delivering the capability our warfighters need requires our current fleet to be ready. Increased operational tempo continues to strain execution of established class maintenance plans. The Navy will no longer attempt to restore all capabilities and add upgrades in a single, lengthy maintenance availability. Instead, the Navy is integrating operations and maintenance into a maintenance continuum that will use smaller, more frequent availabilities to do needed work while increasing operational availability. The Navy is focused on ensuring ships return to service faster through improved planning, innovative contracting acquisition strategies, and investing in our Sailors to make them more efficient, productive and able to contribute to increased readiness."

> "We must fix our maintenance schedules with disciplined planning measured to the hour, because every day a ship sits in dry dock beyond plan is a day we lend initiative to an adversary. To drive this change, we are accelerating a World Class Planning approach which will heavily leverage Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) to transform maintenance from a reactive to a predictive process. The Navy is building 'digital twins' to virtually replicate its ships and simulate decades of wear with the ability to test repair strategies in a virtual environment. Further, these technologies will identify bottlenecks and streamline the supply chain by predicting which parts will be needed months in advance."

> "Additionally, the Navy is implementing an innovative set of contracting strategies like multi-ship sustained partnerships in private shipyards. The Navy is awarding contracts earlier for complex maintenance to increase planning time and material procurement enabled by these innovative tools. The Navy is using additional acquisition tools, including retaining specialized vendors for unique repairs."

> "We will perform this work through PAE Industrial Operations. PAE Industrial Operations combines control of the Navy Regional Maintenance Centers, NAVSEA's Industrial Operations Directorate, the four Navy Shipyards and all Navy Regional Maintenance Centers. This new organizational structure tightly links the contracting functions for maintenance work with the fleet components that must have responsibility for the ships themselves."

> "We must also address repair and maintenance by fully employing our most capable asset — our Sailors. The reestablishment of Shore Intermediate Maintenance Activities will expand their role in performing more advanced maintenance, increasing capacity in peacetime and ensuring continuity of operations in conflict. For this effort to succeed, the Navy and Marine Corps must have the right to repair their own equipment. The DON has systematically undervalued its IP rights during contract formulation, resulting in proprietary lock-in on technical data packages, software, and sustainment data. This has led to excessive non-competitive sole-source awards in follow-on procurement and sustainment, driving up total ownership costs and degrading the Department's organic maintenance capability. We are exploring new mechanisms and existing DFAR rights in technical data to ensure that contractors deliver the information the Department needs and that taxpayers have paid for to make the right to repair a reality."

### Revitalizing the Shipbuilding Industrial Base — Planned Investments (page 35)

> "Between FY18 and FY26, Congress has appropriated more than $20B to support shipbuilding industrial base resilience and expansion. These efforts have helped to alleviate shipbuilder bottlenecks (e.g., blast and paint facilities), improve workforce development (hiring, wages, training, childcare, transportation), and mitigate supply chain risk (distributed shipbuilding, capacity expansion, and lead time reduction)."

> "Congress has consistently appropriated funding in support of increasing industry capacity, and the Navy again seeks that support with a generational increase of $19.3B requested in the PB27 budget from PB26."

### Foreign Investment and Support — MRO authorities (page 37)

> "For Maintenance, Repair and Overhaul (MRO), we propose continuing to leverage FY25 NDAA authorities for 21-day short-term maintenance periods for deployed forces in overseas yards. Experience to date has shown improved readiness."

### Appendix 1 — Resultant Battle Force Inventory — Table A1-5 (page 43)

Total Naval Force Inventory by fiscal year (selected years): FY27 = 288, FY30 = 293, FY35 = 307, FY40 = 357, FY45 = 365, FY50 = 370, FY55 = 387, FY56 = 398. The 355-ship statutory requirement is reached at FY40.

### Appendix 3 — Annual Funding for Sustainment (page 45, charted in Figure A3-1)

The sustainment chart Figure A3-1 plots three stacked components (Manpower, Operations, Ship Maintenance) against the total naval force inventory through FY56. The figure shows sustainment funding ramping from roughly $50B annually at the start of the FYDP to substantially higher levels (the highest annual columns in the FY50s appear to exceed $80B in then-year dollars). Specific dollar figures by component are not legible at the figure's resolution; the budget exhibits in the comptroller's J-Books are the authoritative numeric source.

> "When the life of a ship is extended, the sustainment requirement grows as the age of the ship increases. Moreover, sustainment resources programmed to shift from a retiring ship to a new ship must now stay in place for the duration of the extension. The sustainment requirement grows until equilibrium is reached at the desired higher force inventory, when deliveries match retirements and all resourcing accounts reach steady state at a higher, enduring cost."

### Appendix 4 — Planned Ship Decommissioning, Dismantling and Disposals (pages 46-47)

USS FITZGERALD (DDG 62) — the destroyer used in the first Japan SWARMEX described in the SRF-JRMC public-affairs source — is scheduled for inactivation in FY2030 with disposition "LSA" (Logistics Support Asset), age 35, Expected Service Life 35.

Other notable inactivations within the FYDP that bear on the BDR research:
- USS NIMITZ (CVN 68) — FY2027, dismantle, age 52, ESL 50.
- USS EISENHOWER (CVN 69) — FY2030, recycle, age 53, ESL 50.
- Five Ticonderoga-class cruisers (CG 59, 62, 64, 65, 71) — FY2027-FY2028, all disposition LSA.
- USS ALBANY (SSN 753), USS COMSTOCK (LSD 45), USS NEVADA (SSBN 733), USS TORTUGA (LSD 46), USS RUSHMORE (LSD 47), USS ARLEIGH BURKE (DDG 51) and USS BARRY (DDG 52) — all in the FY2029-FY2031 window.

Ships designated for dismantling during the FYDP are listed in Table A4-2 with the "Ex-" prefix indicating their post-decommissioning status.

### Conclusion (page 39)

> "This plan is a decisive course correction and one that reasserts American maritime power by restoring the capacity, speed, and accountability required to win in an era of great power competition. For too long, we have accepted a system that delivers too little, too late, and at too great a cost."

> "The Golden Fleet Initiative is how we meet that moment. It aligns investments, reforms our acquisition system, and mobilizes American industry to deliver a Fleet that is larger, more capable, and ready to fight tonight."
