# Industrial Rebuild: Betting on Scarce Aerospace and Defence Capacity

### Three unloved franchises I’m accumulating while markets still discount the strategic value embedded in their backlogs

**Industrial Depth, Not Just Defence Budgets**

At this point it’s hardly original to point out that defence budgets are rising again.

**The more interesting question is whether allied countries still possess the industrial depth required to support those ambitions.**

The headlines are everywhere.

Europe is rearming after decades of underinvestment.

The United States is trying to expand naval and munitions output faster than its industrial base comfortably allows.

Australia is investing heavily in structural naval and sustainment capacity driven by AUKUS and Indo-Pacific fleet support, while parts of Asia continue lifting readiness requirements as regional tensions rise.

But military capability ultimately depends on more than procurement announcements. It is also about whether fleets can actually remain operational over long periods of time. Shipyards that can turn out vessels. Engine shops that can perform certified overhauls. Skilled labour, supplier ecosystems, inventories, tooling, repair infrastructure, and the logistical capacity needed to keep large installed fleets functioning.

A lot of that industrial depth was allowed to thin out after the Cold War as procurement intensity faded across much of the developed world. Rebuilding it is slow, expensive, politically awkward, and constrained by real bottlenecks in labour, suppliers, and physical industrial capacity.

The hardest layers sit inside things like advanced naval construction, military propulsion, and certified engine overhaul. Those ecosystems rest on decades of accumulated engineering practice, hard-won regulatory approvals, specialized tooling, dense supplier networks, and highly trained labour pools. It is challenging to scale quickly once they erode.

That changes the character of demand across the sector.

**As time goes on, the economics migrate from one-off procurement toward sustainment intensity, readiness, overhaul and replacement cycles: The long-duration economics of maintaining large operational fleets.**

**Market Still Sees Cyclicals**

Yet equities have not adjusted to this backdrop evenly.

A number of businesses in these ecosystems are still being valued largely through cyclical or legacy lenses, even when the underlying assets are strategically important industrial infrastructure.

As fleets age and remain operational longer, the limiting factor becomes maintenance capacity and industrial throughput rather than the original production order itself.

That disconnect becomes especially interesting once a company has already been through a rough chapter. It might be a failed programme, governance trouble, balance‑sheet strain, or a temporary operational stumble, but the result is the same. Those old problems keep anchoring market perception even long after the underlying business has improved.

**The three companies I discuss in this piece sit in different corners of the same broader problem: Scarce certified industrial capacity tied to ongoing aerospace and defence sustainment.**

One operates inside sovereign naval-industrial workflow and sustainment infrastructure.

Another is embedded in the global engine‑overhaul ecosystems that support some of the world’s largest active aircraft fleets.

The third lives inside military and commercial propulsion systems that run through some of the most strategically sensitive layers of the aerospace supply chain.


What first caught my eye was that t**hese names had already made fresh highs earlier in the year before selling off sharply** **despite operating in a backdrop that should be working in their favour.**

More interestingly, I see longer‑duration strategic and institutional capital patiently building positions in these names and getting real skin in the game.

That divergence is often worth paying attention to because it can reveal a gap between what a business truly is becoming and what the market still thinks it is, based on an older frame.

That is what I am trying to unpack in this piece.

Whether parts of these franchises are being unfairly judged through stale narratives and overly myopic readings of current setbacks, even as the business quality, backlog composition, and strategic relevance continue to develop favourably.

That dislocation tends to present interesting opportunities.

**Three Things Worth Watching**

To make that more concrete, here are a few ideas worth keeping in mind as you read.

1. **Certification:** Approvals from governments, regulators, and OEMs take years to build, cost money to maintain, and naturally narrow the field of qualified participants. They shape the competitive landscape in ways that are not obvious and easy to underestimate from the outside.

2. **Sustainment:** Much of the demand here is not about new equipment at all. It comes from keeping existing fleets operational through maintenance, overhaul, readiness support, life extension work, and replacement cycles. That kind of demand behaves differently from ordinary industrial production because it is tied more closely to operational intensity and fleet availability than to short-term procurement cycles. Once large deployed fleets are deeply entrenched, sustainment demand can persist for decades.

3. **Backlog:** Not all backlog has the same economics. A long-duration availability agreement with a government counterparty behaves very differently from short cycle competitively tendered work, even if both are technically recorded as backlog. The visibility, resilience, and operational integration can vary enormously.

**Not All Defence Exposure Is Equal**

Even inside certified niches, positioning can vary a lot.

Some businesses operate in near sole-source roles. Others compete inside preferred networks or smaller certified pools where rivals exist but within much tighter operational and regulatory boundaries than ordinary industrial markets.

Markets often flatten those distinctions into generic sector labels even though the durability of the underlying economics can be very different.

You see similar simplification in how government exposure gets treated.

**In most industries, customer concentration is treated primarily as a risk.** In defence sustainment and sovereign industrial ecosystems, exposure to government-linked fleets and programmes can also create unusually long tail revenue visibility. That does not remove budget swings or procurement timing risk entirely, but it can make the demand profile much steadier than ordinary transactional industrial work.

That dynamic becomes especially important in environments where fleets are ageing, replacement cycles are delayed, overhaul capacity remains constrained, and governments are trying to rebuild industrial resilience at the same time.

**This piece will be most useful if you do not have much exposure to defence or aerospace sustainment yet, but also do not feel like chasing the obvious primes and large‑cap winners at already stretched valuations.**

There is still a part of the market that offers exposure to the same structural demand drivers without having to pay rich multiples for it.

**The Recognition Gap**

The opportunity here is not just defence spending. It is the market’s slow recognition that these defence industrial assets are now less like cyclical suppliers and more operationally critical.

What makes these businesses worth a closer look is where their true economics actually come from. Keeping existing systems running, operating inside certified bottlenecks that are hard to replicate, and supplying capabilities to fleets, platforms, and sustainment ecosystems that governments and operators cannot easily walk away from.

The recognition gap exists because these transitions rarely happen cleanly.

They reside in businesses that often still carry older narratives shaped by governance issues, programme setbacks, leverage concerns, or earlier cyclical weakness.

Markets are generally good at pricing what is immediately visible.

But they are much less reliable when the underlying economic character of a business changes slowly enough that the old category continues to dominate perception long after the fundamentals underneath have started to evolve.
